We don't have it easy. We're a bunch of normal students trying to share what we know. We expect that we'll make mistakes, but we're also committed to doing everything we can to give you a lecture produced to the best of our abilities. Read on and find out more.

The Production Line

At least six hours of work goes into a single lecture. Here's what happens when we roll up our sleeves and get down to work.

Forming

In the first stage of planning, we gather a team of students with tremendous exposure and experience in the subject matter. We sit down for coffee. Our lecturers get comfortable saying no to each other.

Storming

It all starts with an outline. What goes in, what stays out, what's unclear. Everyone looks at that outline, then it's what's good, and what sucks. Fifteen to twenty revisions later, we have a list of lectures.

Norming

Allocation time. Our lecturers know what they know best, so we lay everything out a la mode buffet. Every lecture belongs to someone. From there, we work out the preparation material. More coffee.

Performing

We go down to headquarters at a top secret location. The on-site support team awaits. There's this big, scary camera they use to do the recording. The footage is edited and corrected, then sent for quality checks.

Quality Checks

A lecture that comes off the production line is checked thoroughly. If we feel it's necessary to get another opinion, we'll even show it to people who are totally unfamiliar with the subject. This isn't just any other "user-generated content". This is the real stuff.

We'll look at what we have and ask ourselves if we can make it better. If the answer is yes, we throw it away and start again. Stuff doesn't go up until the answer is no. Linan Qiu, CEO and Founder of openlectures

Everyone at openlectures shares two things in common: an intimate grasp of their subject matter, and a fervent passion for sharing their knowledge. Everyone works pro bono, after office hours, and on weekends. Everyone loves what they're doing.

  1. We refuse to make publicly available something that hasn't passed through the QC process, no matter the demand. It's time consuming, and it slows us down, but it's what makes us different. It's our responsibility.
  2. We ask ourselves if a student would use it, if a teacher would accept it, and if we would dare put our name on it. We take pride in what we do.
  3. We look at the accuracy of the material, the quality of the footage, and the depth of engagement. All three must be at least satisfactory.
  4. We are prepared to re-look the quality of any lecture at any time. We will not hesitate to pull the lecture and mark it for re-film if necessary.
  5. We value community feedback as an integral part of the process, and will act on it as we would our for own decisions.

The Subject Breakdown

Lectures are monitored throughout the entire process.

Once a lecture has been filmed, it's cleaned up and passed to other lecturers for a look over. When all's good, it's uploaded and given a final review. We're conscious that what we see in earlier stages may differ from the web-friendly product. Once we're ready to unveil the lecture to the world, it's published.

Sometimes we miss out a thing or two and it's only discovered after the lecture has been released. If it's possible for us to provide an explanation, we add it by the side. If the problem's severe, we take the entire lecture down straight away. Either way, we schedule a re-film, big or small.

We receive many suggestions on topics to look into or new areas to expand into. When this happens, we work out how it'll fit and mark it KIV for when we've finished the current production sequence.
